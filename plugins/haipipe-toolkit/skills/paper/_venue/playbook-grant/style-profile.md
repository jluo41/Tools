# Grant Style Profile (imitate this)

Distilled language style and preferences to imitate when writing or editing a grant
proposal (the proposal is the "paper", the funding agency is the "venue"). This is a
STARTER from grant-writing conventions; ENRICH it from the actual FUNDED proposals
stored in `exemplars/` as they are added (pull real sentences and mirror their
shapes). A grant argues for FUTURE work (feasibility plus potential), not completed
work (results plus claims): write "what we will do" and "why it will work".

## Specific Aims / summary

- The Specific Aims (or 概要 / Extended Synopsis) is the proposal's "abstract":
  write it first, refine it last. It carries the gap, the aims, and the payoff.
- Arc: significance and why it matters -> what is unknown -> what we propose ->
  why it will work -> what we will deliver. Significance-first, not method-first.
- End on the contribution and deliverables, not on the technique.

## Gap statement

- One crisp paragraph; the single most important sentence in the proposal.
- Pattern: "Despite progress in [X], [specific gap] remains unaddressed because
  [reason]. This proposal addresses it by [approach], which will [expected impact]."
- Position the gap against the existing literature AND against competing funded
  projects, not just against general background.

## Aims and hypotheses

- Each aim traces to a rationale, then a concrete, testable objective and a
  deliverable; aims are independently valuable (if one fails, the rest still
  deliver) yet logically connected (Aim 1 enables Aim 2).
- Pattern: "Building on [preliminary result], we will [objective]; success yields
  [deliverable]." Each aim names its risk level and a fallback.
- Avoid vague aspirational aims and long undifferentiated aim lists.

## Sentences

- Persuasive but rigorous; declarative; define terms precisely on first use.
- Reviewer-skimmable: bold the key sentence of each section, use numbered lists,
  make the panelist's job easy. Lead each paragraph with its point.
- Minimal hype; no buzzword stacks; one idea per sentence.

## Feasibility / why it will work

- Preliminary data de-risks: cite pilot results, existing datasets, prior
  publications, infrastructure, and team expertise as evidence the plan is doable.
- A timeline with concrete milestones and deliverables IS a feasibility argument.

## Tone & preferences

- Measured, contribution-centered, mission-aware: meet the agency's lens.
- Cite competing FUNDED work to show field awareness; never fabricate amounts,
  publications, or credentials (leave `[AMOUNT]` / `[TODO]` placeholders).

### Per-agency tone notes

- **KAKENHI**: formal Japanese academic style (である調); emphasize 社会的意義 and
  独創性; explicit 年次計画 with concrete yearly milestones; reference KAKEN-funded
  related projects.
- **NSF**: clear direct English; Aim-based bold headings; preliminary-data
  paragraph per aim; Broader Impacts must be concrete and specific, never generic.
- **NSFC**: formal Chinese academic style; 立项依据 positioned at 国际前沿; numbered
  创新点; 研究基础 must cite the applicant's own publications; detailed 可行性分析.
- **ERC**: compelling high-risk / high-gain narrative; self-contained Extended
  Synopsis; WP table with deliverables and milestones; strong PI narrative.
- **DFG / SNSF / ARC / NWO**: state-of-the-art-then-objectives backbone; ARC stresses
  benefit to Australia; NWO stresses knowledge utilisation.
