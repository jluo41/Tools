# JAMA Flagship Introduction -- Section Style Guide

Extracted from JAMA Original Investigation exemplars. Supplements `style-profile.md`.

## Word budget

- 400-800 words (3-5 paragraphs). JAMA introductions are SHORT compared to IS or Nature journals. They set up the clinical problem and state the objective, nothing more.
- Krebs (2018): ~350w, 3 paragraphs. Mathioudakis (2025): ~500w, 4 paragraphs. Cipriani (2026): ~650w, 4 paragraphs. Rotenstein (2026): ~450w, 3 paragraphs.
- No subsections. Always a single flat block titled "Introduction" (implicit; the heading is not printed in JAMA -- the text starts after the abstract).

## Arc

```
clinical problem + burden/prevalence (paragraph 1)
  -> current approaches + their limitations (paragraph 2)
  -> gap: what is not known or not tested (paragraph 2-3)
  -> this study: what we did and how (final paragraph)
```

The arc is **problem-forward, method-light**. The introduction names the clinical problem and its scale, identifies what is missing, and announces the study in one sentence. It does NOT preview findings, declare contributions, or include a literature review.

## Signature moves

1. **Open with a clinical burden statement.** The first sentence states the disease/problem and its magnitude using a concrete number (prevalence, cost, mortality). This is a factual anchoring sentence, not a literature review.
   - "Long-term opioid therapy became a standard approach to managing chronic musculoskeletal pain despite a lack of high-quality data on benefits and harms." [Krebs 2018]
   - "Prediabetes affects approximately 38% of US adults and has a growing global prevalence." [Mathioudakis 2025]
   - "With 280 million people currently affected in the world, major depressive disorder (MDD) is one of the leading causes of global health burden, and it is forecast to increase further by 2050." [Cipriani 2026]
   - "Documentation in the electronic health record (EHR) requires substantial physician time, averaging 2.3 hours per 8 hours of patient care." [Rotenstein 2026]

2. **Build the gap with "however" / "but" / "although" / "yet."** The gap statement appears in paragraph 2 or 3, introduced by a contrastive conjunction. It names what has NOT been done.
   - "Systematic reviews cited by guidelines identified no randomized trials of opioid therapy that reported long-term pain, function, or quality-of-life outcomes." [Krebs 2018]
   - "Although previous randomized trials have assessed digital adaptations of the DPP, none had experimental groups that were fully automated and AI-based and none used a human-led DPP control group." [Mathioudakis 2025]
   - "However, there is no consensus on which pretreatment characteristics can accurately predict efficacy and acceptability of specific antidepressants." [Cipriani 2026]
   - "However, findings have varied across sites, practice settings, clinician types, and specialties... Similarly, there is limited evidence on the association of AI scribes with changes in productivity." [Rotenstein 2026]

3. **Close with a single-sentence study announcement.** The final sentence of the introduction states what "this study" did. It is concise, uses past tense, and names the design.
   - "The Strategies for Prescribing Analgesics Comparative Effectiveness (SPACE) trial was a pragmatic randomized trial that compared opioid therapy vs nonopioid medication therapy over 12 months..." [Krebs 2018]
   - "This pragmatic randomized clinical trial (RCT) was conducted to evaluate whether referral to a fully automated AI-powered DPP is noninferior to referral to a human coach-based DPP..." [Mathioudakis 2025]
   - "In this randomized clinical trial, we compared the PETRUSHKA tool with usual care." [Cipriani 2026]
   - "To address these gaps, this study evaluated the association of AI scribe adoption in the context of real-world introductions of these technologies with changes in total EHR time, documentation time, work outside of work, and weekly visit volume across 5 health systems." [Rotenstein 2026]

4. **No contribution declaration, no findings preview, no roadmap.** Unlike IS journals, JAMA introductions do not enumerate contributions, preview results, or outline the paper structure. The reader moves directly from "this study did X" into Methods.

## Exemplar sentences (shape, not content)

**Clinical burden opener**:
- "[Disease] affects approximately [N]% of [population] and [consequence]." [Mathioudakis 2025]
- "[Activity] in the [system] requires substantial [resource], averaging [quantity]." [Rotenstein 2026]

**Current-approach + limitation**:
- "[Intervention] is the criterion standard for [outcome], demonstrating [number] risk reduction. However, implementation is limited by [barrier]." [Mathioudakis 2025]
- "Human scribes can reduce EHR time, but their scalability is constrained by cost and workforce availability." [Rotenstein 2026]

**Gap statement**:
- "Systematic reviews cited by guidelines identified no randomized trials of [intervention] that reported [long-term outcome]." [Krebs 2018]
- "There is limited literature regarding which clinicians benefit most from [technology]. Similarly, there is limited evidence on the association of [technology] with changes in [outcome]." [Rotenstein 2026]

**Study announcement**:
- "This [design] was conducted to [evaluate/compare/determine] [intervention] [vs comparator] [on outcomes] [in population]." [general pattern across all papers]

## Anti-patterns

- Do NOT start the introduction with a literature citation ("Smith et al found that..."). Start with the clinical problem.
- Do NOT preview findings in the introduction. JAMA saves results for the Results section.
- Do NOT include a contribution list. JAMA has no "our contributions are" paragraph.
- Do NOT include a roadmap sentence ("The remainder of this paper is organized as follows"). JAMA structure is standardized; no roadmap needed.
- Do NOT write more than 4-5 paragraphs. If the introduction exceeds 800 words, it is too long.
- Do NOT define every abbreviation in the introduction. Define abbreviations on first use, but keep the introduction clinically focused, not technically dense.

## Paragraph structure

| Paragraph | Job |
|-----------|-----|
| 1 | Clinical problem: disease/issue + prevalence/burden + why it matters |
| 2 | Current approaches: standard of care or existing solutions + their limitations |
| 3 (optional) | Deeper gap: what specific evidence is missing, what has not been tested |
| 3 or 4 (final) | This study: design + objective, 1-3 sentences |

Total: 3-4 paragraphs. Never more than 5.
