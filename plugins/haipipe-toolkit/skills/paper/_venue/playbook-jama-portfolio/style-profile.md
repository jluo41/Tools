# JAMA-family Style Profile (imitate this)

Distilled language style to imitate when writing or editing a JAMA-family manuscript.
Patterns below are pulled from the real exemplars in `exemplars/` (Schroeder 2019 JAMA
IM; Burns 2024, Kim 2026, Witt 2026 JAMA Network Open). Each template names its source.

## Title

JAMA-family titles are noun phrases, associational, no causal verb. Real patterns:

- "Association of [exposure] From [source] for [population] With [outcome]" (Schroeder).
- "Association Between [factor] and [behavior]" (Witt).
- "Variation in [behavior] by [factor] Contribution" (Burns).
- "Shifts in [behavior] by [factor] for [population], [years]" (Kim).

For ProjB: "Association of Physician Agreeableness With Opioid Prescribing Among ...".

## Key Points box (JAMA Network Open requires it; JAMA IM uses it too)

Three labeled one-liners above the abstract: Question / Findings / Meaning.

- Question: one sentence. "How have patterns of [behavior] by [factor] changed among
  [population] from [years]?" (Kim) / "Can [intervention] decrease [behavior]?" (Witt)
  / "How much [outcome] variation is attributable to [levels]?" (Burns).
- Findings: "In this [design] of [N], [result]." State the design and N, then the
  result with numbers. (Kim, Witt, Burns all follow this.)
- Meaning: "This study suggests that ..." one-sentence implication, cautious.

## Abstract (structured)

Headers in order: Importance / Objective / Design, Setting, and Participants /
Exposures / Main Outcomes and Measures / Results / Conclusions and Relevance.

- Importance opener: clinical problem, then the gap in one clause. "However, little is
  known about how [pattern] varies by [factor] over time." (Kim) / "factors associated
  with it are unclear." (Burns).
- Objective: "To examine the association between [exposure] and [outcome]." (Schroeder)
  / "To assess the association between [factor] and [behavior]." (Witt).
- Design: name it plainly. "This retrospective cohort study ..." (Schroeder) / "This
  repeated cross-sectional study used the Medicare Part D ... dataset ..." (Kim).

## Results sentences

- One result per sentence, past tense, effect in clinical units with 95% CI:
  "(adjusted absolute risk difference, 6.8%; 95% CI, 6.3%-7.2%)" (Schroeder).
- Shares reported from -> to: "increased from 13.8% to 39.6%" (Kim).
- Variance as ranges: "ICCs ranged from 0.57-0.79 for the patient, 0.04-0.22 for the
  anesthesiologist" (Burns).
- Associational verb only: "was associated with". State nulls plainly: "was not
  associated with subsequent changes ... when preexisting changes ... were included"
  (Witt). Never "caused / led to / reduced" for observational data.

## Discussion

- Open with principal findings, restating design + N: "In this cohort study,
  clinician-, hospital-, and patient-level factors had important contributions to
  substantial variation ..." (Burns).
- Then: comparison to prior work -> plausible mechanism (a trade-off, not blame) -> a
  dedicated Limitations paragraph (observational, confounding, generalizability) ->
  Conclusions / clinical relevance.

## Conclusions and Relevance

- One to two sentences, cautious, the takeaway. "The findings suggest that ..."
  (Schroeder) / "These findings suggest the need for ..." (Burns).

## Tone and preferences

- Measured, patient-centered, policy-aware; no hype, no buzzword stacks; one idea per
  sentence; define abbreviations on first use; no econ jargon (not "extensive/intensive
  margin", use "whether prescribed" vs "dose once prescribed").
- Reporting: STROBE (observational); structured abstract + Key Points; CONSORT (trials).
- No-blame framing for ProjB: agreeable approximates saying yes; clinical firmness can
  protect; present as a trade-off, not a fault.
