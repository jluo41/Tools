# JAMA Internal Medicine -- Discussion Style Guide

Distilled from 11 JAMA IM original investigations (Williams 2025, Desai 2026, Kurlander 2026, Fournier 2026, Hirshberg 2025, Berkowitz 2025, Boone 2026, Seamans 2018, Wen 2020, Taylor 2025, Kroenke 2015, Masterson 2025, Zhang 2026).

## Word budget

- 1000-1800 words. JAMA IM Discussions are moderate-length, with more room for clinical interpretation and policy implications than JAMA flagship.
- Observational/policy studies: ~1200-1800 words (4-7 paragraphs + Limitations + Conclusions). Wen 2020 and Seamans 2018 use longer discussions because they explore multiple mechanisms and policy implications.
- RCTs: ~1000-1500 words (3-5 paragraphs + Limitations + Conclusions). Taylor 2025 and Masterson 2025 are more focused on interpreting the primary null result and its clinical significance.

### Contrast with JAMA flagship

- JAMA IM Discussions place greater emphasis on clinical-practice and health-policy implications.
- JAMA IM more frequently includes explicit "Strengths and Limitations" as a labeled subsection (vs JAMA flagship which often folds limitations into a paragraph without a heading).
- JAMA IM Discussions more commonly discuss mechanisms in terms of patient behavior, physician behavior, and system-level factors rather than pure biology.
- JAMA IM has a separate **Conclusions** subsection (with a colored heading and horizontal rule) that is more clearly delineated from the Discussion body than in JAMA flagship.

## Arc

```
P1: Principal findings (restate design + N + main result)
    |
P2-P3: Comparison to prior work + interpretation
    |
P4: Mechanisms / clinical interpretation
    |
P5: Policy implications (JAMA IM signature)
    |
Strengths and Limitations (labeled subsection, 1-2 paragraphs)
    |
Conclusions (labeled subsection, 1-2 sentences)
```

## Paragraph-by-paragraph structure

### Paragraph 1: Principal findings

Opens by restating the design, population, and main result. This is the most formulaic paragraph in the paper.

**Template**: "In this [study design] [of/including/analyzing] [N] [population], [main finding with direction and magnitude]. [Second most important finding]."

Or for studies with multiple main findings: "Our study yielded [N] main findings. First, [finding 1]. Second, [finding 2]. Third, [finding 3]."

Exemplar opening sentences:
- "We conducted a large, retrospective, cohort study comparing the risk of opioid initiation among commercial insurance beneficiaries who were newly exposed to prescription opioids vs prescription NSAIDs through a household member's prescription. We observed a 0.71% absolute higher 1-year risk of prescription opioid use among individuals in opioid households compared with prescription NSAID households." (Seamans 2018)
- "We analyzed associations between Medicaid expansion and opioid-related hospital events using all-payer data on inpatient hospitalizations and ED discharges from 2005 to 2017. Our study yielded 4 main findings. First, we identified a reduction in opioid-related inpatient hospitalizations associated with the post-2014 state implementation of Medicaid expansion. Second, we found suggestive evidence of larger inpatient reductions in states with greater treatment capacity... Third,... Fourth,..." (Wen 2020)
- "In the ENCOMPASS pragmatic, stepped-wedge cluster randomized clinical trial among high-risk patients with sepsis, a multicomponent navigator-led STAR program did not significantly reduce the composite of all-cause readmission and mortality at 90 days." (Taylor 2025)
- "The stepped-care intervention targeted musculoskeletal pain because it is the most common, disabling, and costly of all pain conditions. ... We found that an intervention combining analgesics, self-management strategies, and CBT is effective in reducing pain-related disability, pain interference, and pain severity." (Kroenke 2015)
- "More than 1 in 5 patients hospitalized for decompensated heart failure are readmitted within 30 days, highlighting the need for more effective postacute care strategies. In this large randomized clinical trial involving a racially and socioeconomically and clinically diverse cohort, no significant differences were observed." (Masterson 2025)
- "In this quasi-experimental cross-sectional study of primary care visits, we observed a clear effect of lower time pressure on care patterns related to comprehensiveness of care but not on preventive care provision." (Zhang 2026)

**Signature move**: The Wen 2020 "N main findings" structure is common in JAMA IM for studies with multiple endpoints or policy-relevant dimensions. It provides a roadmap for the Discussion. For studies with a single clear primary finding, a single-sentence opener suffices (Boone 2026: "This quasi-experimental difference-in-differences study examined the outcomes associated with adding a quality metric and financial incentive...").

### Paragraphs 2-3: Comparison to prior work

Contextualizes the findings against prior literature. Uses phrases like:
- "Previous studies on [topic] have used [method]... but none examined [specific gap]."
- "Our findings are consistent with those of [prior study]."
- "In contrast to [prior finding], we found..."
- "Findings from the [current study] are consistent with those in [N] previous trials."

Exemplar:
- "Previous studies on prescription opioids have used MarketScan, but none examined patterns of use within families." (Seamans 2018)
- "Our findings suggest that expanding Medicaid may be a useful strategy for reducing opioid-related hospitalizations. This possible benefit may be due to better management of opioid use disorder in outpatient settings among newly insured individuals." (Wen 2020)

### Paragraph 4: Mechanisms / clinical interpretation

Offers plausible explanations for the observed associations, but with appropriate hedging. Uses conditional language: "may", "could", "one possible explanation is", "this may reflect".

Exemplar mechanism discussions:
- "The association between potential access to household opioids and opioid initiation could be explained by multiple mechanisms. First, family members may share their medications... Second, household opioid use may shape attitudes and norms of opioid use. Third, spousal correspondence of opioid use may be due to homophily." (Seamans 2018)
- "One reason why the STAR intervention may have reduced readmissions in the original IMPACTS trial but not in the ENCOMPASS trial is that the 3 IMPACTS hospitals were urban and highly connected to ambulatory services." (Taylor 2025)

**Signature move**: JAMA IM discussions frequently present multiple competing mechanisms (First... Second... Third...) rather than arguing for a single explanation. This reflects the journal's observational-study emphasis where causal mechanisms are uncertain.

### Policy implications paragraph (JAMA IM signature)

Many JAMA IM papers include an explicit policy-implications paragraph, often near the end of the Discussion body before Limitations.

Exemplar:
- "Our findings have implications for contemporary health policy debate at state and federal levels. An increasing number of states have been adopting Medicaid expansion... Our results provide nonexpansion states additional information to consider in expanding Medicaid." (Wen 2020)
- "Clinicians may need to consider the context within which medications will be used and the potential risk of subsequent opioid initiation by other individuals." (Seamans 2018)
- "These findings suggest that scheduling strategies that reduce time pressure may alter how clinicians allocate attention and effort within visits, particularly for complex patients." (Zhang 2026)

## Strengths and Limitations

JAMA IM almost always uses a labeled subsection: **"Strengths and Limitations"** or just **"Limitations"**. This is a colored subheading, the same style as Methods subsections.

### Structure

Some papers use "Strengths and Limitations" and interleave both:
- "We generalized the 'new-user' design to leverage information on households... The benefit of our approach is the exclusion of households with prevalent use..." (Seamans, strength first, then limitation).
- "A key strength of this study is the use of same-day visits as a plausibly exogenous source of variation in time pressure... Several limitations should also be noted." (Zhang 2026).

Most papers use plain "Limitations" and list them sequentially:
- "Our study is subject to limitations. First,... Second,... Third,... Fourth,..." (Wen 2020, Taylor 2025).
- "The ESCAPE trial has some limitations. First,... Second,... Third,... Fourth,..." (Kroenke 2015).

### Common limitation types (in order of frequency)

1. **Generalizability**: single health system, specific population, geographic restriction.
2. **Unmeasured confounding**: observational design caveat.
3. **Data limitations**: claims data lacking clinical detail, self-report bias, missing variables.
4. **Measurement**: proxy outcomes, administrative data vs clinical assessment.
5. **Temporality/causality**: cross-sectional design, reverse causation.
6. **Power/sample size**: for null results, whether adequately powered.

### Exemplar limitation sentences

- "Although we controlled for a number of potential confounders, we also assessed the sensitivity of our findings to unmeasured baseline differences between the opioid and NSAID groups." (Seamans 2018)
- "Second, Medicaid expansions were not randomly assigned. Although our difference-in-differences methods control for some sources of bias, we cannot rule out the possibility that some other change occurred differentially in expansion vs nonexpansion states at the same time as the expansion." (Wen 2020)
- "First, the COVID-19 pandemic occurred during the trial. We accounted for time-varying effects in the analysis, but we cannot rule out residual confounding related to the COVID-19 pandemic." (Taylor 2025)

**Signature move**: Limitations are numbered ("First,... Second,... Third,...") in most JAMA IM papers. Each limitation is followed by a brief acknowledgment of how it was partially addressed or why it is unlikely to change the main conclusion.

## Conclusions

JAMA IM has a clearly separated **Conclusions** subsection with its own heading (preceded by a colored horizontal rule). It is short: 1-3 sentences.

### Pattern

"[Design recap] + [main finding restated in plain language]. [One sentence of forward-looking clinical/policy implication or call for future research]."

### Exemplar Conclusions

- "Our study underscores the importance of Medicaid expansion in altering how patients with opioid use disorder interact with the medical system. To the extent that Medicaid expansion reduces avoidable hospitalizations, the expansion may be providing individuals with more patient-centered pathways into treatment." (Wen 2020)
- "Although we cannot rule out shared risk factors, such as clinician preference, prescription drug monitoring, or genetic predilection, our results suggest that opioid prescribing decisions may need to take into account the context within which medications will be used and the potential risk of subsequent opioid initiation by other individuals." (Seamans 2018)
- "In this stepped-wedge cluster randomized clinical trial, our multicomponent navigator-led STAR program delivered to sepsis survivors for 90 days after discharge did not improve the composite of 90-day all-cause readmission and mortality. Future work should evaluate whether the intervention improves mortality through increasing appropriate rehospitalization." (Taylor 2025)
- "A stepped-care intervention that combined analgesics, self-management strategies, and CBT benefited OEF/OIF/OND veterans with chronic musculoskeletal pain in terms of pain-related disability, pain interference, and pain severity." (Kroenke 2015)
- "The Mighty-Heart randomized clinical trial observed similar overall outcomes for health status and 30-day readmission in the MIH and TOCC alone groups, suggesting no added benefit of MIH... However, the exploratory findings that MIH may have supported better health status in younger patients underscores the importance of further research." (Masterson 2025)
- "In this quasi-experimental cross-sectional study of primary care visits in a large health system, lower visit time pressure was associated with differences in how care was delivered... These findings suggest that scheduling strategies that reduce time pressure may alter how clinicians allocate attention and effort within visits." (Zhang 2026)

**Signature move**: Conclusions restate the design name explicitly ("In this [design],..."). For null-result trials, the Conclusions state the null clearly without spin, then suggest what future research should examine.

## Signature moves across the section

1. **No new data in Discussion**: Discussion never introduces new results or analyses not presented in Results.

2. **Hedging language**: "may", "could", "suggests", "appears to", "one possible explanation". JAMA IM is particularly cautious about causal language for observational designs.

3. **Numbered findings in the opener**: Many JAMA IM papers use "Our study yielded N main findings" followed by a numbered list.

4. **Multiple competing mechanisms**: Rather than advocating for one mechanism, JAMA IM papers present several plausible explanations with equal weight.

5. **Clinical practice framing**: Implications are framed for the internist reader: what should clinicians consider, what should be monitored, what policy changes might follow.

6. **No "In conclusion" in the body**: The Conclusions subsection is separate. The Discussion body does not end with "In conclusion..." -- it ends with the Limitations subsection.

## Anti-patterns

- Starting Discussion with a mechanism or interpretation before restating the principal findings.
- Using causal language ("our study proves", "this intervention reduced") for observational data.
- Including new analyses or results not presented in the Results section.
- Writing Limitations as a single run-on paragraph rather than numbered points.
- Ending the Discussion body (before Conclusions) with clinical implications rather than Limitations.
- Omitting the explicit design name from the Conclusions paragraph.
- Overstating clinical implications for null-result trials.
- Writing Conclusions that are longer than 3 sentences.
- Using "In summary" or "In conclusion" as the final Discussion body paragraph (the separate Conclusions heading handles this).
