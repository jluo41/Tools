# JAMA Internal Medicine -- Introduction Style Guide

Distilled from 11 JAMA IM original investigations (Williams 2025, Desai 2026, Kurlander 2026, Fournier 2026, Hirshberg 2025, Berkowitz 2025, Boone 2026, Seamans 2018, Wen 2020, Taylor 2025, Kroenke 2015, Masterson 2025, Zhang 2026).

## Word budget

- 500-900 words (3-5 paragraphs). JAMA IM introductions are compact but slightly longer than JAMA flagship (which runs ~400-600). JAMA IM tolerates more clinical context and policy motivation.
- Observational/policy studies: 4-5 paragraphs (~700-900 words). Seamans 2018 and Wen 2020 both use 4-5 substantive paragraphs building the clinical/policy case.
- RCTs: 3-4 paragraphs (~500-700 words). Taylor 2025 and Kroenke 2015 use 3-4 paragraphs, spending more space on the intervention rationale and prior trial evidence.

### Contrast with JAMA flagship

- JAMA IM introductions run slightly longer, with more clinical/epidemiological context and more explicit policy framing.
- JAMA IM papers more frequently cite public health burden (cost, mortality statistics, ED visits) with specific numbers in the opening.
- JAMA IM papers are more likely to frame the gap in terms of clinical practice or health policy rather than pure scientific knowledge.

## Arc

```
P1: Clinical burden + prevalence/cost/mortality (the "crisis sentence")
    |
P2: What is known (prior evidence, guidelines, existing approaches)
    |
P3: What is NOT known (the gap -- explicit "however" or "but" pivot)
    |
P4: What we did (study aim, design overview, contribution)
    |
[Optional P5: Subquestions or secondary aims]
```

## Paragraph-by-paragraph structure

### Paragraph 1: The burden sentence

Opens with the clinical condition/problem at population scale. Uses concrete numbers: prevalence, cost, mortality, ED visits. Establishes urgency.

Exemplar opening sentences:
- "Opioid prescribing in the US increased 300% from 1991 to 2009 and totaled 246 million dispensed prescriptions in 2015." (Seamans 2018)
- "The opioid epidemic has reached crisis levels in the US. Approximately 400 000 people have died from opioid overdoses over the past 20 years." (Wen 2020)
- "Chronic pain is a critical health problem that affects approximately 100 million US adults. Chronic pain costs the United States an estimated $635 billion each year in medical treatment and lost productivity." (Kroenke 2015)
- "Sepsis is a common life-threatening condition defined by organ dysfunction due to a dysregulated response to infection." (Taylor 2025)
- "Heart failure is the leading cause of hospitalization among older adults in the US and has the highest 30-day all-cause readmission rates (20% to 25%) among Medicare beneficiaries." (Masterson 2025)
- "Primary care in the US is under strain due to a growing shortage of primary care clinicians relative to rising demands for increasingly complex care for an aging population." (Zhang 2026)

**Signature move**: The very first sentence names the condition AND provides a quantitative anchor (N affected, cost, percentage, or mortality). No throat-clearing. Exception: policy-focused papers (Boone 2026) may open with a policy axiom instead ("A central tenet of value-based care is that health care spending should be tied to the quality of care provided") and deliver the numbers in the second sentence.

### Paragraph 2: What is known

Reviews prior evidence, guidelines, or existing interventions. Establishes that the field has tried things or has partial knowledge. Citations are dense here (often 5-10 references in one paragraph).

Typical moves:
- "Several studies found that..." followed by a summary of consistent findings.
- Naming specific guidelines or policy initiatives (e.g., "The Veterans Affairs pain management strategy was initiated in 1998").
- Noting that evidence from prior work is "modest" or "limited" or addresses a different population.

### Paragraph 3: The gap (the "however" pivot)

This is the most critical paragraph. Explicitly states what is unknown, using an adversative conjunction: "However," "Despite this," "Nevertheless," "Yet," or "but."

Exemplar gap sentences:
- "However, little is known about how Medicaid expansion affects opioid-related hospital use." (Wen 2020)
- "Despite this effort, no intervention studies to treat chronic pain in veterans of the Iraq and Afghanistan conflicts have been reported, to our knowledge." (Kroenke 2015)
- "However, it is unknown whether the STAR program is effective and scalable across geographically and resource-diverse hospitals." (Taylor 2025)
- "however, they have not previously been directly compared in a randomized clinical trial." (Masterson 2025)
- "However, most studies have focused on increased time pressure; none to our knowledge has used a quasi-experimental study design to investigate whether reducing time pressure produces opposite or distinct effects." (Zhang 2026)

**Signature move**: The gap sentence often includes the phrase "to our knowledge" or "little is known about" or "it is unknown whether."

### Paragraph 4: What we did

States the study aim and provides a one-sentence summary of the design. Uses first person plural ("We").

Exemplar aim sentences:
- "We quantified the association between potential access to opioids resulting from a new prescription started by a household member and subsequent new use of prescription opioids by other family members." (Seamans 2018)
- "Using state-level hospital discharge data from 46 states and the District of Columbia from 2005 to 2017, we assessed the association between Medicaid expansion and rates of opioid-related ED visits and inpatient hospitalizations." (Wen 2020)
- "To assess this, we used electronic health record (EHR) data from a large integrated health system and leveraged whether subsequent same-day visit slots were filled as a source of quasi-experimental variation in visit time pressure." (Zhang 2026)

**Signature move**: The aim sentence often bundles design, data source, and research question into one or two sentences. The verb is associational ("assessed the association", "examined whether", "compared") rather than causal ("determined the effect of").

### Optional Paragraph 5: Secondary aims or additional context

Some papers add a final short paragraph listing secondary research questions or subgroup analyses they explored.
- "We further explored whether associations varied across subgroups of age, geographic region, potential indications, refill availability, days' supply, and by year and opioid dose." (Seamans 2018)

## Signature moves across the section

1. **Drop cap**: JAMA IM uses a decorative drop cap for the first letter of the introduction. The first word is typically 2-4 syllables. Authors do not need to format this; the journal applies it.

2. **Numbers early**: The opening paragraph almost always contains at least two specific numbers (prevalence + cost, or prevalence + mortality). This is a JAMA IM signature -- the burden must be quantified, not just asserted.

3. **Policy hook**: JAMA IM introductions frequently name a specific policy (ACA, Medicaid expansion, VA pain management strategy, CDC guidelines) or a specific guideline. This clinical-policy framing distinguishes JAMA IM from basic-science or methods-focused journals.

4. **"We" introduction**: The study aim paragraph always uses "we" rather than passive voice. This is consistent across all papers examined.

5. **No hypotheses stated**: Unlike some clinical journals, JAMA IM introductions do not state formal hypotheses (H1, H2). The aim is framed as a research question or an examination of an association.

## Anti-patterns

- Opening with a definition rather than a burden/urgency sentence. ("Opioids are a class of drugs that..." -- too textbook.)
- Spending more than one paragraph on what is known before getting to the gap. Keep the review tight.
- Stating a causal hypothesis for observational studies. Use "association" language.
- Including methods details (sample size, dates, statistical approach) in the introduction beyond a brief design mention in the aim paragraph.
- Writing a gap sentence without the explicit adversative pivot word ("However" / "but" / "Despite").
- Ending the introduction with a hypothesis instead of a study aim.
- Including results previews in the introduction.
