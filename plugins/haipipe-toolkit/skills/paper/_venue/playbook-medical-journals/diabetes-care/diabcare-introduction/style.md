# Diabetes Care -- Introduction Style Guide

Distilled from 10 Diabetes Care papers: Galindo 2026 (RCT/Brief), Reaven 2026 (TTE), Bergenstal 2026 (GRADE), Lehmann 2026 (ML/Brief), Zheng 2025 (NLP), Kahkoska 2025 (e-Letter), He 2026 (e-Letter), Godneva 2026 (TIR), Ajjan 2026 (gDAC), Dupenloup 2026 (cost-effectiveness).

## Word budget

- Original Article: 600-1000 words (3-5 paragraphs).
- Brief Report: 150-300 words (1 paragraph, integrated with the article start).
- e-Letters/Observations: ~200-400 words (1-2 paragraphs, no heading; prose flows directly into methods/results).

### Contrast with JAMA

JAMA IM introductions run 500-900 words (3-5 paragraphs) with more explicit policy framing. Diabetes Care introductions are slightly longer for Original Articles, with more room for technology context and ADA Standards of Care positioning. Diabetes Care does not use a drop cap.

## Arc

```
P1: Clinical/technology context + prevalence/burden
    |
P2: What is known (prior evidence, guidelines, existing approaches)
    |
P3: What is NOT known (the gap -- "however" / "but" / "yet" pivot)
    |
P4: What we did (study aim, design overview)
    |
[Optional P5: Secondary aims or scope statement]
```

Brief Reports compress this to a single paragraph covering context -> gap -> aim.

## Paragraph-by-paragraph structure

### Paragraph 1: Context and burden

Opens with the clinical domain or technology at population scale. Establishes relevance. Can start with a known benefit, a prevalence statement, or a technology description.

Exemplar opening sentences:
- "Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) has been shown to improve glycemic control and reduce hypoglycemic events in people with type 1 diabetes (pwT1D) (1,2)." (Reaven).
- "Glycemic management metrics derived from continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) are increasingly recognized as important therapeutic targets (1,2)." (Bergenstal).
- "Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) has transformed diabetes management, which is increasingly used in diabetes clinical and research settings (1)." (Zheng).
- "Glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) remains an important glycemic metric that informs glycemic management in diabetes (1,2)." (Ajjan).
- "Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) devices have undergone substantial improvements in recent decades and are now broadly available worldwide (1)." (Godneva).
- "Although type 1 diabetes is one of the most prevalent chronic medical conditions diagnosed in childhood, relatively limited data exist on the cost-effectiveness of emerging diabetes technologies in this age group." (Dupenloup).

**Signature move**: The first sentence names the domain (CGM, HbA1c, diabetes technology) and establishes its clinical relevance. Citations are dense (typically 1-3 references in the opening sentence). Unlike JAMA, Diabetes Care does not always lead with a quantitative burden (prevalence, cost, mortality); it often leads with a technology or clinical context statement.

### Paragraph 2: What is known

Reviews prior evidence, technology developments, or guideline recommendations. ADA Standards of Care is frequently cited here.

Typical moves:
- "We previously demonstrated, using real-world data from the Department of Veterans Affairs Health Care System (VHA), that initiation of CGM in pwT1D may also reduce emergency room or hospital admissions for hypoglycemia..." (Reaven).
- "The American Diabetes Association 'Standards of Care in Diabetes' has recommended the inclusion of standard AGP reports for glucose assessment (6)." (Zheng).
- "CGM is essential for creating the ambulatory glucose profile (AGP) and providing data on time above, in, and below range; glycemic variability; and average glucose." (Zheng).

### Paragraph 3: The gap ("however" pivot)

The most critical paragraph. States what is unknown using an adversative: "However," "but," "Yet," "Despite."

Exemplar gap sentences:
- "However, there are very limited data on the potential benefits of CGM use in pwT1D for all-cause mortality (10)." (Reaven).
- "However, the application of NLP to extract data from CGM reports remains unexplored." (Zheng).
- "However, for a given average glucose (AG), there are large interindividual variations in HbA1c related to differences in red blood cell (RBC) turnover and glucose handling (3)." (Ajjan).
- "However, large-scale longitudinal studies investigating time spent in different glycemic ranges... are lacking." (Godneva).
- "The cost-effectiveness of care models which augment CGM with RPM remains unexplored." (Dupenloup).

**Signature move**: The gap sentence often includes "are lacking", "remains unexplored", "limited data exist", or "little is known about". Always uses an explicit adversative pivot word.

### Paragraph 4: What we did

States the study aim and provides a design overview. Uses first person plural ("We").

Exemplar aim sentences:
- "We therefore used the large T1D population and the comprehensive observational medical records within the VHA to emulate a 'target trial' to estimate the effect of CGM initiation on all-cause mortality at 1-4 years in pwT1D." (Reaven).
- "Thus, we aimed to evaluate the accuracy of extracting CGM data using NLP." (Zheng).
- "Here, we analyze data obtained from CGM devices collected as part of the Human Phenotype Project (HPP)..." (Godneva).
- "In this study, we estimate the effects of CGM augmented with RPM on diabetes-related complications, mortality, and cost-effectiveness compared with SMBG and CGM alone in a pediatric population..." (Dupenloup).

**Signature move**: The aim sentence connects the gap to the design using a causal conjunction ("therefore", "thus", "accordingly") or a temporal marker ("Here, we..."). The verb is descriptive ("aimed to evaluate", "used X to estimate", "analyze data from") rather than causal.

### Paragraph 5 (optional): Secondary aims or scope

Some papers add a scope-narrowing or secondary-aim paragraph:
- "In the current work, we focus on how glycemic profiles are associated with each of the GRADE medications in the context of the changes in HbA1c..." (Bergenstal).

## Signature moves across the section

1. **ADA Standards of Care citation**: Most papers cite the current ADA Standards at least once in the introduction, positioning the study within the clinical guideline framework.

2. **Technology-first framing**: Unlike JAMA (which leads with disease burden), Diabetes Care papers often lead with the technology (CGM, ML, NLP) and its established benefits, then pivot to the gap.

3. **Abbreviation discipline**: Every abbreviation is defined on first use in the introduction, even if it was already defined in the abstract (the two are treated as independent scopes).

4. **No hypotheses stated**: Diabetes Care introductions do not state formal hypotheses (H1, H2). The aim is framed as a research question or an evaluation.

5. **First person plural**: "We" is standard throughout: "We aimed...", "We therefore used...", "We analyze...".

6. **Dense citations**: The introduction is citation-heavy, typically 8-15 references across 3-5 paragraphs.

## Anti-patterns

- Opening with a textbook definition ("Diabetes is a chronic condition defined by...") without clinical relevance.
- Spending more than two paragraphs on what is known before getting to the gap.
- Stating a causal hypothesis for observational studies. Use "association" language.
- Including methods details (sample size, dates, statistical approach) beyond a brief design mention in the aim paragraph.
- Writing a gap sentence without an explicit adversative pivot word ("However" / "but" / "Yet").
- Including results previews in the introduction.
- Omitting ADA Standards of Care when the study topic intersects a current recommendation.
- Using passive voice for the aim statement. Use "We" actively.
