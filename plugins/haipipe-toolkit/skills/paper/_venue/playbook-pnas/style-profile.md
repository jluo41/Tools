# PNAS Style Profile (imitate this)

Distilled language style and preferences to imitate when writing or editing a PNAS
manuscript. This is a STARTER from PNAS conventions; ENRICH it from the actual papers
stored in `exemplars/` as they are added (pull real sentences and mirror their
shapes).

## Significance Statement

- ~120 words, on the title page, read before the Abstract by the handling editor and
  reviewers.
- Lay-accessible: a second-year graduate student in any field should understand the
  stakes; avoid specialist jargon.
- Arc: problem in field-general terms -> the advance (what is now possible,
  measurable, or understood) -> why it matters beyond the immediate specialty.
- Not parallel with the Abstract; reviewers compare them and parallelism is a red
  flag.
- Evidence-bounded: "establishes X" beats "may contribute to understanding X"; do not
  hedge, do not overstate. The Significance Statement is cited when claims are later
  disputed.

## Abstract

- <=250 words, unstructured prose.
- Significance-first: state the broadly significant advance, then what was done and
  found.
- Legible to scientists outside the immediate specialty; inherits framing from the
  Significance Statement.

## Contribution framing

- One crisp statement of the broadly significant advance, not a list of incremental
  results.
- Frame importance as cross-disciplinary implication, not field-internal importance.
- A method that is novel elsewhere is an enabler in Methods, not the headline.

## Sentences

- Declarative, significance-forward; define terms that cross a disciplinary boundary
  on first use.
- Position the advance for a broad scientific readership, not only the specialty.
- Minimal hype; no buzzword stacks; one idea per sentence.

## Discussion

- Restate the broadly significant advance first, then cross-disciplinary
  implications, then a dedicated limitations paragraph, then conclusion.

## Tone & preferences

- Measured, significance-centered, cross-disciplinary aware.
- Rigor and reproducibility weighted over novelty-per-se; show a careful evidence
  chain with broad applicability.
- Reporting: method executed by its own standards; explicit data and code
  availability; domain reporting standards (MIAME, ARRIVE, CONSORT, etc. as
  applicable).
